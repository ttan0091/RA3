## The Interview Process

### Discovery Phase

The interviewer read the user's existing notes on AI and coding, identifying themes around:
- Tension between AI assistance and deep learning
- Personal investment in skills like vim being "devalued"
- Questions about documentation and knowledge flywheels

### Key Interview Questions Asked

1. "You mention AI handles the 'how' — but what happens to the people who used to be the 'how' experts?"
2. "You learned vim, invested real effort. Now you're prompting. What exactly feels lost?"
3. "If everyone offloads learning to AI, who writes the documentation AI learns from?"
4. "You said your NATS blog gets 23% traffic from AI. How does that change your relationship to writing it?"
5. "Where does taste come from if you haven't done the work?"

### Tensions Explored

- Meta-thinking may require deep understanding at some point
- The knowledge flywheel problem: AI learns from humans who learned the hard way
- Skill devaluation vs. new forms of expertise

## Core Thesis
AI handles the "how," humans focus on the "why." Obsession moves from implementation to user experience and product taste.

## Key Ideas Explored

### The Knowledge Flywheel Problem
- AI learns from documentation and blogs written by people who learned the hard way
- If everyone offloads learning to AI, who writes the next wave of documentation?
- Your NATS blog gets 23% traffic from AI — you're contributing to the flywheel

### The Personal Loss
- You learned vim, invested real effort to get faster
- Now you're just prompting — that skill feels devalued
- Writing the blog forced you to understand deeply, fill knowledge gaps, validate AI-assisted code

### The Reframe
- Coding becomes like grocery shopping — picking ingredients (NATS vs Kafka) based on the dish (product)
- But experience still matters — the best product thinkers are those who've "cooked" enough to have intuition
- The new fun: experimentation, A/B testing, real-time user feedback loops

### The Tension
- Meta-thinking may require deep understanding at some point
- Taste comes from having done the work
